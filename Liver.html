Liver Calcification: Causes, Symptoms, and Treatment

Which organ plays multiple important roles in our bodily functions, including but not limited to boosting metabolic processes, bolstering immune function, and detoxifying the blood and body? Your liver, of course! It is one of the most vital organs in the human body, but it is often susceptible to damage, infections, and other complications. One such complication is liver calcification. This condition can lead to compromised liver function, which can either have mild or severe symptomatic presentations.

Introduction

In this article, we shall understand what liver calcification is, what its various causes are, and some of its common symptoms. We shall also learn about how to diagnose this problem, some liver calcification treatment options, as well as various preventive measures you can take to prevent complications.

What is Liver Calcification?

In simple terms, liver calcification is a liver injury. Scarring shows up on the liver wall and can be caused due to some parasitic infection, inflammation, or lesions. This scarring in turn leads to small stones getting accumulated in the biliary tract of the liver. Over time, these stones become calcified and hard. Because of this deposit, the liver is unable to detoxify the body and complete proper functioning, and the stones also become too hard to naturally be flushed out.

Causes of Liver Calcification

As mentioned above, liver calcification can occur due to many factors, which can cause some long-term damage. Some of the most common causes are listed below:

  • Infections: Scar tissues formed due to the body recovering from damaging infections like schistosomiasis (parasitic origins) or tuberculosis (bacterial origins) can accumulate calcium and lead to calcification of the liver.
  • Metabolic Disorders: Conditions like hemochromatosis (iron overload) and Wilson’s disease (copper overload) cause the body to store too much iron or copper. This excess mineral buildup can present in the liver as well, and over time, some of these minerals can harden into calcium deposits.
  • Biliary Tract Diseases: Issues in bile flow, caused due to bile duct stones or primary bile cholangitis, can have a damaging effect on the bile duct and the surrounding liver tissues. This damage sometimes triggers scar tissue formation, which can calcify over time.
  • Vascular Disorders: Budd-Chiari syndrome (blocked hepatic veins) blocks blood flow to the liver, which may lead to liver damage and scarring, which in turn may lead to calcification.

Liver Calcification Symptoms

In the initial stages, the patient may not even notice any obvious symptoms, and hence this condition often gets misdiagnosed. Some common severe symptoms of liver calcification are listed below. If you notice or undergo any of these symptoms, consult a doctor immediately.

  • Right Flank Pain: Pain in the right flank, especially during vigorous exercise, is very common for patients who are suffering from severe calcification of the liver.
  • Jaundice, accompanied by yellow eye mucosa: If your liver is not functioning properly, your skin, urine, and eye mucus will turn deep yellow, indicating jaundice and improper liver function.
  • Digestive Disorders: The calcified nodules can often lead to obstruction of the bile duct, which can cause digestive problems.
  • Fever, Chills, or Convulsions: Such intense symptoms only manifest when liver damage due to calcification is very severe.

Diagnosing Calcification of the Liver

There are several methods employed in diagnosing liver calcification, some of which are listed below:

  • Imaging Tests: Ultrasound and CT Scan are used to detect calcifications in the liver.
  • Blood Tests: No direct way to diagnose liver calcification, but can test for underlying causes of liver problems.
  • Biopsy: A small tissue sample from the liver is observed under a microscope if other tests are inconclusive.

Liver Calcification Treatment

The treatment options for liver calcification vary depending on the severity of the condition. In many cases, liver calcifications are harmless and don’t require specific treatment, but more severe cases may require medical intervention.

  • Slight Calcifications: Can be managed by lifestyle changes and healthy eating habits.
  • Moderate Calcifications: Medication or drugs to treat underlying conditions and manage symptoms.
  • Severe Symptoms: Surgical removal may be necessary in cases of extreme pain or bile duct obstruction.

Preventive Measures

To prevent liver calcification, it is important to maintain overall liver health through lifestyle modifications:

  • Limit foods high in cholesterol to reduce the risk of gallstones.
  • Maintain a balanced diet with lean protein, white meats, vitamins, and minerals.
  • Avoid overeating and maintain a regular eating schedule.
  • Sleep adequately and avoid alcohol and tobacco.
  • Engage in regular exercise and drink plenty of water.
  • Get regular check-ups and keep track of your medical records with a PHR app.
